Course Project in the Coursera Data Science Specialization Regression Models - Analysis of the mtcars data set.

Executive Summary

In this course project, data set mtcars is analyzed with regards to the two questions whether an automatic or manual transmission is better for a cars gas mileage (miles per gallon) and to quantify the mpg difference between automatic and manual transmissions. The column mpg in the mtcars data set is selected as outcome. The following predictor variables are chosen: factor(am) as a two-level factor variable (am=0 for manual and am=1 for automatic transmission); wt measuring a cars weight in units of 1000 lbs; hp measuring a cars horse power in units hp; and factor(am)*wt as interaction term between variables am and wt. Separate linear regression fits are conducted for a comparison of mpg as outcome and factor(am) and wt as predictors and factor(am) wt as interaction term and a second comparison with mpg and factor(am) and hp as outcome and predictor variables, respectively. A general decrease of miles per gallon can be observed with an increase in horse powers, as expected which is independent of the selected group, i.e.whether automatic (am=1) or manual transmission (am=0) is being assumed. An average downward shift of about 5 miles per gallon in mpg can be seen when switching from am=1 to am=0. In the other comparison, separate linear fits occur with different slopes for the two groups am=1 and am=0 when mpg is fitted versus wt. This differentiation is due to a relevant interaction term factor(am) wt. Automatic transmission (am=1) appears favorable for higher gas mileage when weights are smaller while for increased weights a cross-over happens where manual transmission (am=0) starts yielding higher mpg values. This crossover happens at a weight of around 3000 lbs.

Data Processing

The data set mtcars includes the data of of 32 vehicle and 10 column variables. We calculate the mean, median, standard deviation, minimum and maximum for each of the three key continuous variables in this analysis, mpg, wt, and hp, for each group manual transmission (am=0) and automatic transmission (am=1). The quick analysis of the data highlights a trend of larger mpg values for automatic transmission (am=1) versus manual transmission (am=0), i.e.on average 17 mpg for am=0 and 24 mpg for am=1. However, this also needs to be seen in the context of larger average weight of vehicles of 3769 lbs (am=0) versus 2411 lbs (am=1), respectively, while a vehicles horsepower shows a smaller average of 126 hp for am=1 versus 160 hp for am=0 while there is also a larger spread of hp values in the first case.

Linear Regression Analysis

In the selection of proper variables for this analysis, we examine the p-values of the linear regression fits of outcome mpg to the final selection of continuous predictor variables wt and hp together with the factor variable factor(am). This is highlighted in following model likelihood fits using the R tool anova where all linear regression models as described above up are listed. Each of the above variables are highly significant (p-values less than 0.05 for 95%-confidence level). While the interaction term factor(am) wt also proves to be highly significant, the interaction term factor(am) hp ends up being omitted for the final analysis.

The Appendix summarizes the above findings and analysis in a series of different plots:

  • Figure 1: Two histograms are shown to demonstrate the distribution of mpg values in the data set for the two groups automatic transmission (am=1) and manual transmission (am=0).

  • Figure 2: The data points for the two regression models are highlighted in red for group am=0 (manual) and in black for am=1 (automatic). The left-hand plot shows two linear regression fits with different slopes with mpg as outcome versus wt as predictor (black and red lines) due to the included interaction term, factor(am) wt. The right-hand plot shows two linear regression lines with equal slope when fitting mpg versus hp. An overall shift of average mpg values occurs for automatic versus manual transmission at constant hp values.

  • Figures 3: Figure 3 shows the distribution of normalized residuals for each of the above two regression models. Normalization is done with respect to the standard deviation as computed assuming a t-student distribution due to the small sample size (only 32 vehicle brands compared).

  • Figure 4: These two plots compare residual values (y-axis) with fitted values (x-axis) of each model. A clear separation of the data of the two groups am=0 and am=1 can be seen towards lower and higher mpg values, respectively.
